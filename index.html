Could hemp be the future of 3D printing?

Hemp
as a bioplastic?

Bioplastics are lightweight and malleable building materials generated from renewable resources such as corn, vegetable, and even hemp oil as opposed to non-renewable fossil fuels or natural gas. Unlike regular plastics and some other forms of bioplastic, hemp-based bioplastic can be both biodegradable and recyclable! Prototypes being sold today are made of a biodegradable thermoplastic derived from short hemp fibers (Hempzoo, 2020).

Hemp is an incredibly versatile and renewable resource, especially when compared to other conventional textile and building materials. With specific regards to the plant‚Äôs fibers which are used for bioplastics, hemp matures in just 60-90 days (Hemptech, 2018). The quick turnaround of this cultivation makes hemp a great contestant for bioplastics, especially at scale. Furthermore, some industrial hemp producers such as ‚ÄúHemp technologies global‚Äù are able to achieve approximately the same fertility as a high-yielding crop of wheat, making it a very profitable and scalable renewable resource (Hemptech, 2018).

With regards to land use, hemp is very advantageous as it can grow in a more compact environment than common crops such as corn, all while also not needing any pesticides due to its natural properties (3D-Fuel, 2020).

Biodegradability:

For the purpose of 3D printing, hemp fibers can be converted into a bioplastic PLA (Polylactic acid) which is then used as a building material for 3D printers. PLA is a naturally biodegradable plastic, so the fact that hemp fibers can seamlessly be synthesized into this format is a great selling point. In addition, when hemp is converted into PLA it has a natural clear/brown color. This neutral appearance and consistent color throughout the PLA means that hemp-based products do not need to use dyes created from dangerous chemicals which can disrupt the biodegradability of this bioplastic (Hempzoo, 2020).

Currently available hemp-based filament?

Hemp-based PLA filaments for 3D printers are still in the very early phases of development and mass production, but there exists one company which is producing a filament that consumers can purchase today. Entwined claims itself as ‚Äúthe first and only commercially-available 3D printing filament produced from USA-grown and processed industrial hemp (3D-Fuel, 2020).‚Äù Entwined‚Äôs product is compatible with nearly all consumer 3D printers as it comes in several standardized gauges, and can print at a full range of 180-210 degrees Celsius. Entwined states that their filament is produced in Fargo, North Dakota and the hemp they source is grown legally in the United States.

As for the price, a spool of 500 grams of filament currently sells for around $40 and is available for purchase only in the United States. In comparison, a 1-kilogram spool of non-renewable PLA usually sells for around $20 on Amazon. That means that the price for hemp-based PLA is roughly 4x the cost of non-hemp PLA. However, it is worth noting that the production of this bioplastic is still in a very early phase and that the creators of Entwined have expressed their desire to reach a competitive price once producing filament at scale.

The future of hemp-based
3D printing?



So what is the grand vision of 3D printing with hemp-based PLA filament? Well, there are many great uses for 3D printing which could be improved upon by adopting biodegradable bioplastics:

  • Rapidly prototyping custom parts and tools for engineering.

  • Creating custom medical equipment for personalized surgeries and recovery.

  • Assisting with agriculture by producing fine-tuned fertilizer bricks which slowly dissolve.

  • Use in construction, perhaps even entirely building homes out of hemp.



Yes, quite possibly hemp-based filaments could be used to produce homes and other large-scale construction projects. In 2018 a company called ICON, which labels themself as a robotics construction company based in Austin, Texas, designed and build a home that was entirely 3D printed. The goal of their project is to produce housing fit for developing nations that can be rapidly prototyped and constructed from one material. Their prototype which was built in the United States is entirely up to code and inhabitable (Kieger, 2018).
